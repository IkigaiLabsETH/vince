## Appendix (original essay, verbatim)

It’s 3 a.m. again, the blue glow of my screens cutting through the dark like it has for decades. Strong coffee gone cold beside me. Pulled up an old chart tonight—some forgotten angel deal from the early 2010s, one of those quiet ones that compounded nicely while the world chased crypto moons and meme stocks. But that’s not what kept me up. It was an email that resurfaced in my inbox archive, from back in ‘98 or ‘99, right after WebTV featured on the Apple homepage. One of those “congrats, man” notes from a college buddy, followed six months later by a casual ask for a “small bridge loan” to cover his startup’s runway.

We’d pioneered something wild back then, streaming TV over the internet when most folks were still on dial-up. Apple even featured us later, but in the moment? Tribal brain kicking in hard—wanting witnesses, recognition, that pat on the back from the pack. I told a few people. Felt invincible.

And then the gravity shifted.

Look, I’ve survived bubbles—the dot-com melt-up and crash, the ‘08 housing mess where I had to force-sell some positions during redemptions, the COVID flash drop that wiped out LiveTheLifeTV overnight. But the real scars? They came from the human side after those step-function wins. The ones where the digits roll and suddenly you’re not grinding anymore.

Remember that feeling—the first time the old life feels gone? I do. Vividly. For me, it was post-WebTV. The mammal brain doesn’t care about smart. It wants celebration. Witnesses. So I let a little slip. Told family. Close friends. Hell, even posted something vague on early X (back when it was Twitter, and I was one of the ‘07 holdouts). Nothing flashy—just hints. A nicer vacation photo.

First came the envy. Subtle at first. “Must be nice.” That one stings because it’s envy in three words. From people who’d cheered when it was just a pipe dream. Supported the grind when exit velocity was hypothetical. Then separation hits, and the reptilian hierarchy updates. They cope. “You got lucky.” Or the classic for guys like me: “Timing, man—anyone could’ve ridden that wave.” Bet you didn’t see that coming from your oldest crew.

I had one buddy—known him since Quiksilver days, surfing dawn patrols before work. Solid guy, or so I thought. After the exit, texts doubled. “Quick idea—you fund, I run it, 50/50?” Extractors show up like clockwork. Family too. That “small loan” email? From a cousin I’d helped before. Turned into a gift, as they always do. Write it off mentally, or it eats you.

Worse were the moral police. “If I had your success, I’d give back more.” Or judging the modest upgrades—a better coffee setup, quieter office. “You changed, bro.” No, I didn’t. I just stopped pretending to scrape by. But misery loves company. They drag you into the crab bucket if you let them.

And the relationships... success costs you people. Bezos ain’t chatting with high school pals anymore. Same here. Contacts shrink without fireworks. Some drift. Others trivialize: “Yeah, but it was the Microsoft machine that made it big.” Joking jabs at your “bad points.” Cold shoulders at gatherings. If you’re even-keel, no rubbing it in, you see whose egos popped. Wolves in disguise all along. They liked you relatable. Ahead of you, even. Your old life made theirs feel solid.

I wasted time trying to win some back. Apologizing for momentum. Validating their insecurity while burning mine. Emotional poverty. Took years to accept: better contacts replace them. Quiet winners who get it.

But the real killer? Lifestyle creep. That silent wealth destroyer.

We all do it after the big win. Dopamine demands instant upgrades. One wild vacation turns into needing them quarterly. Nice car? Suddenly the wardrobe doesn’t match. Restaurants upgrade. Vacations balloon. Before you know it, a breakout year becomes a money pit.

Seen it wreck athletes—Mike Tyson blowing hundreds of millions on tigers and tubs, entourage bleeding him dry. Antoine Walker earning $108 million in the NBA, bankrupt fast from cars and crew. 60% of NBA players broke five years post-retirement, per old studies. NFL worse—78% in two years, some say. Short careers, peak earnings young, then permanent lifestyle on temporary income.

Lottery winners too. Curse of the lotto—sudden windfall, visibility tax. Strangers begging. Family rifts over loans. Regrets pouring in: “Wish I’d never won.” One guy in India hit $3 million equivalent, posted a video saying he regretted it— inundated with asks.

Startup folks? Same trap. Sell for eight figures after dilution, think you’re set. Then creep hits. Private jets because “time is money.” Multiple homes. Staff. One founder I know—sold his SaaS for mid-eight, upgraded everything. Two years later, grinding again to feed the machine. Converted freedom into chains.

Me? Guilty. Post-exit, bought that car. Felt good... until it needed a garage to match. Then the place needed upgrades. Activation dangerous. Permanent lifestyles brittle—paper-thin error room in bad years.

Learned the hard way: delay everything. After a win, one-year moratorium on upgrades. One modest celebration—party or trip. Then lock it down. Let dopamine settle. You know it’s over when you stop refreshing the account.

Invest instead. Turn windfall into assets spitting passive income. Real estate. Dividends. Quiet compounders. Only upgrade when assets pay for it fully. Rental cash flow covers the house? Pull trigger. No more time traded.

Real flex isn’t consumables that photograph well—cars, watches, tables, bottles. That’s consumption. True wealth: time freedom. Options. Turning down flights because you don’t need the deal. No alarm clock—why need a fancy watch? People come to you. No mortgage owning you.

Instagram can’t post time. That’s the invisible edge.

Warren Buffett nails it—lives in the same $31,500 house from ‘58. Drives modest cars (hail-damaged deals, per his daughter). Steak at Gorat’s, Cherry Coke, hash browns. No flash. Compounding undisturbed.

Me now? Stealth all the way. Underestimated on purpose. In rooms, convey same bracket minus 20%. They think $200k when it’s more. Protects focus. Privacy. Cleaner relationships—no altered incentives.

Visibility is a tax. Human tax. Don’t pay unless you’re unshakeable.

Kind of wild, huh? Reminds me of Scion days—Steinhardt humming along low-key while others blew up loud.Regrets? Yeah. Exited some angels too early—classic me, fearing execution risk. Could’ve held longer. But the big one: not stacking quieter sooner. Told too many. Upgraded too fast.Maybe wrong on some details—memory fuzzy after decades. But felt true then.

Now? Stack in silence. Egos distract. Build conviction.

True friends cheer quietly. Share wins with them. Rest? Let ‘em underestimate.

We’ll see. Markets humble us all eventually. Keep reading filings.

Strong coffee helps.

Stay hungry. Stay foolish. But quiet.
