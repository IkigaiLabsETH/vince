## Appendix (original memo, verbatim)

If you’ve been paying attention to robotics lately, you’ve probably heard the big names—Tesla’s Optimus, Figure AI’s flashy demos, Boston Dynamics flipping around. But there’s a company in Austin that’s taking a different approach, and honestly, it feels like the one most likely to actually ship useful robots soon: Apptronik. They’re building Apollo, a 5’8”, 160-pound humanoid designed to do the jobs nobody wants—hauling boxes in warehouses, moving parts in factories, the kind of repetitive heavy lifting that wrecks human backs. It can carry 55 pounds, runs for four hours on a battery, and the goal is to get the price under $100k, eventually closer to $50k. That’s not sci-fi; that’s “we could start seeing these in real facilities this decade” territory.

Apptronik spun out of the University of Texas at Austin’s robotics lab in 2016. The founders—Jeff Cardenas (CEO), Nick Paine (CTO), and Luis Sentis—cut their teeth working on NASA’s Valkyrie robot for the DARPA challenge. They basically took everything they learned building a robot meant for space and asked: what if we made something that could actually work in a warehouse today?

They spent years on NASA contracts, perfecting compact, efficient actuators (the motors that make the legs and arms move smoothly and safely). They’ve built 15 different systems along the way, including an upper-body bot called Astra for tricky manipulation tasks.

By 2023 they decided it was time to go all-in on a full commercial humanoid. In 2025 they also launched a side venture called Elevate Robotics to build bigger, stronger machines that go beyond what humans can physically do.

Most humanoid hype focuses on crazy agility or perfect hand dexterity. Apptronik went the other direction: reliability, safety, and cost. Apollo uses linear actuators instead of the usual fancy (and expensive) gear systems. It’s built to be safe around people, easy to maintain, and—crucially—cheap enough that companies will actually buy fleets of them.

They’ve also locked in big partnerships to make the AI side actually useful. Google DeepMind is plugging Gemini models straight into Apollo, so it can handle new objects it’s never seen before or follow verbal instructions. Demos from 2025 showed it doing basic household chores, but the real focus is industrial work. They’re working with Texas Instruments on motor control, Jabil on manufacturing scale-up, and even running pilots with Mercedes-Benz in car factories.

The funding tells you how seriously people are taking them. They raised a $350 million Series A in early 2025 (with Google joining the round), pushing the valuation toward $5 billion. By late 2025 they were in talks for another $400 million-plus. Total raised is now north of $778 million, with backers including Nvidia, Mercedes, and even crypto DAOs like XMAQUINA giving retail investors a way in. More importantly, they’re already making real revenue—more than they’d raised in earlier rounds—which is rare in this space.

Jeff Cardenas calls it “the space race of our time,” and yeah, that’s a bold statement, but look at the competition:

Tesla has the hype and valuation, but deployment is still slow.

Figure AI has massive funding and big AI focus.

Boston Dynamics has the best movement, but they’re not rushing commercial products.

Agility’s Digit is solid for warehouses, but narrower scope.

Apptronik’s edge is pragmatism. They’re not trying to build the flashiest robot; they’re trying to build the one logistics and manufacturing companies will actually deploy at scale. Their bet is that the market (estimated at $38 trillion by 2035 by ARK Invest) won’t go to the robot that can do backflips—it’ll go to the one that shows up on time, doesn’t break, and pays for itself.

They’re ramping up production in new Austin facilities, planning upgrades to Apollo this year, and eyeing longer-term markets like elder care and even space (thanks to those NASA roots). Challenges remain—supply chains are tricky, getting the AI to handle real-world chaos takes time—but with the funding, partners, and focus they have, Apptronik feels like the company that might actually deliver the first million useful humanoids.

In a field full of dazzling demos and big promises, Apptronik is playing the long game. And right now, that looks like the smart bet.
