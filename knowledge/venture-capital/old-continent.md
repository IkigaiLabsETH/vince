A quiet realization in the age of trillion-dollar AI bets.

I’ve been investing in crypto for over a decade now. Long enough to watch cycles come and go, hype inflate and deflate, and fortunes made (and lost) on nothing more than conviction and timing.

Lately, though, I’ve found myself staring at charts that have nothing to do with Bitcoin or Ethereum. Charts of venture capital flows. Unicorn counts. 

Valuation gaps.
**
And one image keeps coming back: that bubble chart from a couple years ago, showing the U.S. with hundreds of billion-dollar private tech companies, China close behind, and Europe... a distant third. A small cluster, mostly fintech, dwarfed by the American giants like OpenAI, SpaceX, and xAI.

This isn’t schadenfreude. It’s a warning. And perhaps a lesson.

The Stark Numbers Don’t Lie

As of late 2025, the U.S. boasts around 750-800 unicorns — privately held startups valued at $1 billion or more. China has 300-plus. Europe, including the UK? Roughly 150-200 combined.

Total valuation tells an even grimmer story. American unicorns alone command trillions in paper wealth, fueled by the AI boom: OpenAI at $300-500 billion private valuation, Anthropic, xAI, Databricks — monsters that Europe can only dream of.Europe’s top private tech plays? Revolut in fintech, maybe a few AI upstarts like Mistral. Solid companies. But no trillion-dollar contenders. No category killers sucking up global capital like black holes.

Venture funding reflects this. **

In 2025, U.S. startups raised well over $150 billion in VC. Europe? Around $50-75 billion. A fraction. Q3 alone: U.S. dominated with $80+ billion. Europe scraped $17 billion. It’s not getting better. The gap is widening.

**Fragmentation: Europe’s Original Sin**

Start with the basics. Europe isn’t a single market. It’s 27 countries, each with its own laws, taxes, labor rules, and regulators.

Want to scale a startup across borders? Good luck navigating GDPR in one country, the AI Act’s risk classifications in another, varying stock option taxes, and a patchwork of visa regimes that make hiring global talent a nightmare.

In the U.S., you build once for a unified 330-million-person market. In Europe, you build 27 times. This fragmentation kills momentum. Startups hit regulatory walls early. Expansion costs soar. Unit economics suffer.

**Contrast with Bitcoin. **

Decentralized by design. No borders. No permission needed. 

Send value anywhere, instantly, without asking Brussels or Berlin.

Crypto thrives on Europe’s flaws.

**Capital Drought and Risk Aversion**

European VC funds are smaller. Much smaller. Top funds raise hundreds of millions; U.S. giants routinely close $5-10 billion vehicles.

Why? Institutional money — pensions, endowments, insurers — flows cautiously in Europe. Risk aversion runs deep. Better a safe 4% bond than a moonshot that might return 100x or zero.

Late-stage funding? Scarcer still. The “scale-up valley of death” is real. Many promising European companies relocate headquarters to the U.S. for bigger rounds. Exits compound the problem. Europe’s public markets are fragmented, less liquid, less tolerant of growth-at-all-costs stories. Nasdaq welcomes loss-making tech darlings. European exchanges? Not so much.

Result: Capital recycles slowly. Returns lag. The flywheel spins slower.

Bitcoin? No exits needed. No IPO gatekeepers. 

Just pure market pricing, 24/7. Liquidity for all.

**Talent Drain and Cultural Caution**

Europe produces world-class engineers and researchers. Top universities. Deep talent in math, physics, cryptography.

But where do they go? Silicon Valley. Or remote for U.S. firms.

Why? Better pay. Stock options that actually matter (thanks to favorable taxation). A culture that celebrates risk, failure, and ambition.

Europe’s labor laws protect workers — admirably — but stiffen mobility. Stock options taxed as income? Brutal.

Founders feel it too. Build big in Europe, face brain drain. 

Many European-origin companies now call the U.S. home.

Crypto flips this. Blockchain talent clusters globally. 

Europe has strong pockets — Switzerland’s Crypto Valley, Estonia’s e-residency pioneers. But the network is borderless.

**Regulation: Protection or Straitjacket?**

Don’t get me wrong. GDPR protects privacy. The AI Act aims for responsible development. But compliance costs crush startups. Resources that could fund engineering go to lawyers. MiCA regulates crypto — clearer than U.S. chaos — but adds hurdles. Bitcoin was born in regulatory gray zones. It thrives on permissionless innovation. Europe regulates first, innovates second.

The Bitcoin Contrast: Borderless, Ruthless, Unstoppable

Here’s the irony.

While Europe struggles to birth the next OpenAI, Bitcoin has become a $1.7 trillion asset class. No headquarters. No CEO. No national champions needed.

It scaled globally because it ignored borders.

In 2025, Bitcoin outperforms most tech stocks year-to-date in relative strength at times, holds as digital gold amid uncertainty.

Crypto market cap rivals Europe’s tech unicorns combined — many times over. Blockchain startups? Europe has some — Ledger in France, strong DeFi plays. But few unicorns. The big ones (Chainalysis, etc.) often U.S.-based.

Yet the protocol layer dominates without favoring any continent. 

**Long-Term Optimism, Short-Term Skepticism**

I’m optimistic about Europe. Deep talent. Strong research. Growing funds. Initiatives like the Capital Markets Union could help.2025 showed progress: More unicorns minted than recent years. AI focus sharpening.

But structural fixes take decades. Fragmentation won’t vanish overnight. Risk appetite shifts slowly. Europe excels at incremental innovation. Fintech. Greentech. Solid mid-tier companies.

But the trillion-dollar disruptors? The ones rewriting industries?

Those require bold capital, unified markets, and a tolerance for spectacular failure. Until then, Europe remains cooked — not dead, but simmering on low heat. Bitcoin reminds us: True innovation often bypasses nations altogether.

If you’re investing, think globally. Think permissionless.

The next big thing might not need Europe’s approval to succeed.

It already doesn’t.
