Money is the operating system of human civilization. For most of recorded history, that operating system ran on a single line of code: scarcity enforced by physics. Gold was heavy, rare, and impossible to counterfeit at scale. That single constraint created the closest thing humanity ever had to honest accounting. Empires rose and fell, but as long as the ledger could not be inflated at will, time preference stayed low, savings compounded across generations, and civilization could build cathedrals that took centuries to finish.

Then, in the 20th century, we quietly deleted that line of code.

## Context

We replaced physical scarcity with legal fiction. Central banks were granted the privilege of creating money ex nihilo, first under the pretense of ‚Äúemergency,‚Äù then as permanent policy. The result was not stability; it was the greatest wealth transfer in history‚Äîfrom those who produce and save to those who control the printing press and borrow first. Every monetary expansion since 1971 has been a slow-motion expropriation of everyone holding yesterday‚Äôs dollars, euros, or yen.

Inflation is not a side effect. It is the business model.

The world now runs on a form of money that loses purchasing power by design. This single change cascades through every layer of society. When money decays, time horizons collapse. Long-term investment gives way to short-term extraction. Politicians promise benefits today and send the bill to children who cannot vote. Corporations load up on debt because repaying tomorrow‚Äôs obligations with tomorrow‚Äôs cheaper dollars is the rational play. Universities raise tuition faster than inflation because student loans are guaranteed by the same entity that prints the currency. Real wages stagnate for fifty years while asset prices‚Äîstocks, bonds, real estate‚Äîdetach from reality and orbit the expanding money supply.

## Main

No committee can vote to increase it. No president can order more to be printed during a crisis. No central bank can rescue a failing regime by devaluing the savings of its citizens. The rules are set in immutable code and secured by an energy-backed consensus mechanism that turns electricity into inviolable property rights.

This is why nation-states will eventually treat Bitcoin as a strategic reserve asset: not because they love decentralization, but because the alternative is watching rival powers accumulate a form of money they cannot create or confiscate at will. The same dynamic that once made gold the settlement layer of empires now applies to proof-of-work mining and unspent transaction outputs.

Gold was always physical Bitcoin with terrible software‚Äîslow to move, expensive to audit, impossible to divide below certain thresholds, and vulnerable to central custody. When capital accumulation is once again possible across generations, families think in centuries rather than election cycles. When the unit of account is predictable, contracts become trustworthy again, and strangers separated by oceans can coordinate without intermediaries skimming 5‚Äì15 % off every transaction.
