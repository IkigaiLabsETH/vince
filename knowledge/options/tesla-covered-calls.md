Why Covered Calls Are Particularly Effective on Tesla Right Now

A covered call involves owning at least 100 shares of the underlying stock and selling one call option contract against those shares. You collect the premium immediately. If the stock price remains below the strike price at expiration, the option expires worthless, you keep the premium, and you retain your shares. If the stock rises above the strike and the option is exercised, your shares are called away at the strike price—but you still keep the premium, effectively selling the shares at a higher realized price.

In France, income from option premiums and any capital gains realized upon assignment are treated as capital gains on securities. They are subject to the flat-rate tax (Prélèvement Forfaitaire Unique, or PFU) of 30% (12.8% income tax + 17.2% social charges). Reporting is straightforward: declare net gains on Form 2042 (lines 3VG/3VH), using the broker’s activity statement CSV. All USD amounts must be converted to EUR using the Banque de France’s official daily exchange rates.

Losses can offset gains in the same year and be carried forward for up to ten years. The PFU remains the default regime in 2025, though high-income households may occasionally benefit from opting into the progressive income tax scale.

Risks and Mitigation StrategiesT

he primary risks are well-known:

- Opportunity cost: If Tesla surges dramatically (e.g., on a major Robotaxi announcement), shares may be called away, capping upside.

- Downside exposure: The premium provides only partial cushion against declines.

- Volatility contraction: A sudden drop in implied volatility after a catalyst can shrink future premiums.

Mitigation includes limiting position size (typically no more than 25% of the portfolio in a single name), actively rolling winning positions (buying back the short call and selling a new one farther out in price or time), and maintaining disciplined strike selection guided by probability tools.

Institutions often manage gamma exposure dynamically; retail traders achieve similar outcomes through vigilant monitoring of news flow and upcoming events.

The Institutional “Recycling Machine” and Parallels to Michael Saylor’s PlaybookProfessional trading desks treat covered calls as systematic yield-generation programs. The mechanics are simple and mechanical:

- Collect premium upfront → bond-like income stream.

- If the call expires worthless → keep premium and shares.

- If assigned → deliver shares but keep premium; then immediately repurchase shares to reload for the next cycle.

This recycling creates predictable post-expiration demand, often manifesting as mild upside pressure in the 24–48 hours after major option expirations—particularly noticeable in high-volatility assets like TSLA and Bitcoin.

Michael Saylor’s strategy at MicroStrategy offers a fascinating macro parallel: issuing debt (selling future volatility to bond markets) to fund Bitcoin purchases, effectively turning MSTR stock into a leveraged Bitcoin call option. As of December 2025, the company continues large-scale accumulation, with recent purchases exceeding $980 million in a single week.

Both approaches monetize conviction in an underlying asset by systematically harvesting volatility premiums—Tesla holders sell time value in options; Saylor sells long-term uncertainty to credit investors.
